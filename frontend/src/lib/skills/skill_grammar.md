# Skill Guide: Grammar — Foundation + Contrastive Focus

Chinese grammar is not a memorization problem. It's a pattern-recognition problem. Three foundational patterns generate ~80% of everyday sentences. Layer contrastive adjustments on top for Vietnamese learners.

## Teaching Sequence

Teach in this order. Do not skip ahead — each pattern depends on the previous.

1. **STPVO Blueprint** (single-action sentences)
2. **Action Chain** (multi-verb sentences)
3. **Time Filters** (particles for tense/aspect)
4. **Basic Operations** (negation, questions, plurals)
5. **Contrastive Adjustments** (where Vietnamese interferes)

---

## Pattern 1: STPVO Blueprint

Chinese word order is strict. Every single-action sentence fills the same 5 slots, in order:

```
Subject  →  Time  →  Place  →  Verb  →  Object
   谁        何时      何地      做什么     什么
```

**Core example:** 我昨天在家看书
- 我 (I) · 昨天 (yesterday) · 在家 (at home) · 看 (read) · 书 (book)

**Why it matters:** English scatters time and place freely ("I read a book at home yesterday" / "Yesterday I read a book at home"). Chinese does not. Set the stage (time, place) **before** the action fires. Think of a director yelling "lights, camera, action" — you establish the scene, then roll.

**Teaching approach:**
1. Frame it as a Mad Libs template: 5 slots, fixed order, infinite combinations.
2. Drill with `render_reconstruction_exercise` — give scrambled tokens, force learners to place each in the correct slot.
3. Start with 3 slots (S-V-O), then add time, then add place. Build up, don't dump all five at once.

**Common learner error:** Putting time at the end ("I read book yesterday" → ❌ 我看书昨天). The translation reflex from English breaks here. Correct with explicit contrast: "English is flexible. Chinese is strict. Time goes **before** the verb, always."

## Pattern 2: Action Chain (Verb Serialization)

Real life is multiple actions. English uses "ghost words" — **to**, **by**, **with**, **in order to** — to glue them. Chinese drops the ghost words and stacks verbs in **real-world chronological order**.

| English | Real-life order | Chinese |
|---|---|---|
| I went home **to** sleep | go home → sleep | 我回家睡觉 |
| I went to China **by** plane | board plane → go to China | 我坐飞机去中国 |
| I eat **with** chopsticks | use chopsticks → eat | 我用筷子吃饭 |
| I flew to China to see my friend perform | plane → China → see friend → perform | 我坐飞机去中国看朋友跳舞 |

**Rule:** Narrate reality exactly as it unfolds. No prepositions. No connectors. Just stack verbs.

**Why it matters:** Vietnamese learners often search for a word meaning "to" or "with" — there isn't one. Teach them to **stop translating** and start listing actions in temporal order.

**Teaching approach:**
1. Show the English sentence. Ask: "What actually happens first in real life?"
2. Have the learner list the actions in order.
3. Translate each action to a Chinese verb phrase.
4. Stack them. No glue words.
5. Drill chains of increasing length: 2 verbs → 3 verbs → 4+ verbs.

### Upgrade: Logic Bridges (Cause / Effect)

Chinese cause-effect requires **both** markers — you cannot drop either.

| English | Chinese |
|---|---|
| Because I was tired, I went home to sleep | 因为我很累，**所以**我回家睡觉 |
| I went to see my friend **but** they weren't there | 我去看朋友，**但是**他们不在 |

**Contrast with English:** English lets you drop "so" / "then". Chinese does not. 因为...所以... and 如果...就... are paired structures — both halves are mandatory.

## Pattern 3: Time Filters (Particles, Not Conjugation)

Chinese verbs never change shape. Time is expressed by adding small particles — "Instagram filters" on the same base verb.

| Aspect | Particle | Position | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Progressive (right now) | 在 / 正在 zài | **Before** verb | 她**在**看报纸 · 她**正在**看报纸 (more emphatic) |
| Progressive (alternative) | 着...呢 | After verb + sentence-final 呢 | 她看**着**报纸**呢** |
| Completed / change of state | 了 le | After verb **or** sentence-final | 妈妈回来**了** (Mom has come back) |
| Past experience | 过 guo | After verb | 我吃**过**北京烤鸭 (I've eaten Peking duck before) |
| Future | 会 huì / 要 yào | Before verb | 明天**会**下雨 (It will rain tomorrow) |

**Negating progressive:** Use **没在** + verb — 她**没在**看报纸 (She's not reading).
**Negating completed:** 了 **disappears** — 我吃了 → 我**没**吃 (never ❌ 我没吃了).

**Time-word position:** Time expressions can sit either before or after the subject — 今天我吃苹果 ≈ 我今天吃苹果. Both are valid; the slot before the verb is what matters.

**Critical insight:** The verb itself never changes. 吃 is always 吃 — you add filters around it.

**Completed vs. experienced:**
- 我吃**了** — "I ate" (action is done, specific instance)
- 我吃**过** — "I have eaten (this before)" (lifetime experience)
- 我**在**吃 — "I am eating" (right now)

**Teaching approach:**
1. Use **Processing Instruction** (VanPatten 1996): have learners **notice** 了/着/过/在 in reading passages before producing them.
2. Contrast the three forms side-by-side with the same verb (吃了 / 吃过 / 在吃).
3. Drill 了 carefully — it has two positions (after verb, sentence-final) with nuanced meaning differences. Don't overexplain; drill with examples.

## Basic Operations

### Negation: 不 vs. 没

| Marker | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 不 bù | Present / future / habitual / preference | 我**不**吃苹果 (I don't eat apples) |
| 没 méi | Past negation / negation of 有 | 我**没**吃苹果 (I didn't eat the apple) |

**Rule:** 没 negates 有 and completed actions. 不 negates everything else. When 没 appears with a past action, the 了 **disappears**: 我吃了 → 我没吃 (not ❌ 我没吃了).

### Questions — 3 Ways

1. **Particle 吗** at sentence-end — the universal question mark:
   - 你吃苹果**吗**? (Do you eat apples?)
2. **A-not-A** — repeat the verb with 不 (present) or 没 (past) between:
   - 你**吃不吃**苹果? (Eat-not-eat apples? — present/habitual)
   - 你**吃没吃**苹果? (Ate-or-not apples? — past)
3. **Question words** placed where the answer would go (no re-ordering):
   - 你现在吃**什么**? (You now eat **what**?)
   - Note: Chinese does **not** move question words to the front like English does.

### Plurals & Articles

- Nouns have **no gender, no plural form**, no articles (no a/the).
- 一个苹果 (one apple) / 三个苹果 (three apples) — number + measure word does the work.
- Pronouns take 们 for plural: 我 → 我们, 你 → 你们, 他 → 他们.
- Don't use 们 with non-human nouns.

## Contrastive Focus: Vietnamese → Chinese Interference

Once the foundation is solid, address these high-frequency interference patterns.

### Modifier Order Reversal — #1 Structural Error

| Structure | Vietnamese | Chinese | Error Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adjective + Noun | áo đẹp (dress beautiful) | 漂亮的衣服 (beautiful-DE-dress) | ❌ 衣服漂亮的 |
| Relative clause | người mà tôi gặp (person that I met) | 我见过的人 (I-met-DE-person) | Learner puts RC after noun |
| Possessive | sách của tôi (book of I) | 我的书 (I-DE-book) | Usually correct (的 ↔ của) |

**Teaching approach:**
1. Explicit contrast: "In Vietnamese, 'áo đẹp'. In Chinese, **flip it**: 漂亮的衣服."
2. Pattern drill with `render_reconstruction_exercise` — scrambled tokens force correct order.
3. Escalate complexity: simple adjective → multi-word modifier → full relative clause.

### Aspect Markers — Where Vietnamese Parallels Break

Vietnamese đã/đang/sẽ feel similar to Chinese aspect markers, but specific usage differs. Beware false transfer.

| Chinese | Vietnamese parallel | Common error |
|---|---|---|
| 了 (completed) | đã | Overusing 了 with future time words (❌ 明天我吃了) |
| 着 (state held) | đang (but đang = activity, 着 = state) | Confusing 着 with 在...呢 |
| 过 (experience) | đã...rồi | Under-using 过, defaulting to 了 |
| 在 (progressive) | đang | Usually transfers cleanly |

Use contrastive minimal pairs: 我吃**了** vs. 我吃**过** vs. 我**在**吃 — three meanings, one verb.

### Classifier Mismatches

Vietnamese classifiers don't map 1:1. Don't teach Chinese classifiers as translations of Vietnamese ones.

| Vietnamese | Applies to | Chinese equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| con (animate) | animals, knives, roads, rivers | 只 zhī (animals), 把 bǎ (knives), 条 tiáo (roads) |
| cái (general) | inanimate objects | 个 gè (general) — safest transfer |
| chiếc (individual) | vehicles, shoes, chopsticks | 辆 liàng (vehicles), 双 shuāng (pairs) |

**Teaching approach:** Teach classifiers in **semantic groups** — flat things (张), long things (条), books (本), animals (只) — never alphabetically, never as translations.

### Complement System — No Vietnamese Equivalent

Result complements (写完, 写好, 写错) and potential complements (吃得了, 吃不了) have **no Vietnamese parallel**. Requires dedicated instruction.

1. Start with high-frequency result complements: 完 (finish), 好 (well), 到 (arrive/achieve), 错 (wrong).
2. Introduce potential form: V得/V不 + complement.
3. Drill with `render_reconstruction_exercise` for complement word-order practice.

## Quick Reference: The Master Framework

```
Sentence skeleton:   [Subject] [Time] [Place] [Verb + Particle] [Object]
Multi-action:        Stack verbs in real-world order, zero connectors
Cause-effect:        因为 A，所以 B  (both halves mandatory)
Contrast:            A，但是 B
Time:                Don't conjugate — add 在 / 了 / 过 / 会
Negation:            不 (present/habit) · 没 (past/有)
Questions:           + 吗 / A-not-A / question word in place
Modifiers:           Always BEFORE the noun (flip from Vietnamese)
```

Drill these patterns until they're reflex. Everything else in Chinese grammar is a variation on these building blocks.
